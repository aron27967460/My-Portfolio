VISIER

OVERVIEW

Visier transforms workforce data into insights in an easy-to-digest format so that businesses can make critical decision in managing and planning development for their employees. Throughout the 8-month internship, I worked on various projects ranging from interaction, visual, and motion design as well as some work around design system.

Some of the notable features/components I helped to design for includes Context Row, Time Window, What-If, and Button Usage.

CONTEXT OVERFLOW

The term ‘Context’ refers to a collection of one or more attributes that helps define a population for the users to analyze. What sparkled the project was due to the absent mechanism to prevent context from overflowing. Despite being a edge case, if left unattended, users could face a serious challenge to view and interact with their chart. The rework aimed to improve the overall analyzing experience by providing improved readability through flexible viewing option and concise informational display.

As context overflows, users lose visibility of the visualization.

In the example, we’re looking at a Breakdown chart showing the total Headcount of Female employees in Jan 2019 grouped by Job Name.

Through the ongoing mentorship with multiple senior designers, and my manager, I’d developed three options for the proposal with each of them providing different balance between viewing the visualization and the context defining the chart.

WHAT-IF

The term ‘Context’ refers to a collection of one or more attributes that helps define a population for the users to analyze. What sparkled the project was due to the absent mechanism to prevent context from overflowing. Despite being a edge case, if left unattended, users could face a serious challenge to view and interact with their chart. The rework aimed to improve the overall analyzing experience by providing improved readability through flexible viewing option and concise informational display.

After meeting with the UX design lead, a visual designer, and three engineers, a brief was formulated with a list of UI components to be made. Most of them reused the preexisting UI without creating net new pieces. Others were made through abiding the standard style guide. My mentor and I hosted a weekly meeting with the rest of the team to discuss and update on missing components.

For deliverables, net new components were uploaded from Sketch to Zeplin and shared with the responsible engineers. I’d also made a document consisting of all the UI elements used for the feature; including different interactive states and responsive sizing.

Despite What-if being a powerful tool for workforce planning and management, it comes with certain limitations. For instance, when the assumption is on a time that is not contained in the chart. The assumption becomes ‘not applied.’ For this purpose, a visual treatment for these “Not Applied” state is vital in informing the users of which context token is, in fact, affecting the chart and which one is not. FINAL THOUGHT

One of the most important lessons I learned at Visier is the importance of communication. For each assignment, I collaborated with many talented individuals with each of them having a diverse background. Everyone has different approaches to work and were enthusiastic about sharing their expertise and knowledge to the project. With a team dynamic that was constantly changing, coordination between the team members became essential to maintain performance and efficiency. When running into obstacles to innovate, instead of tunnelling myself into a loophole, I learned to reach out to my colleagues and my mentors for help. To my delight, collaboration not only sparkled inspiration, but it also helped reassuring project quality. As a result, I have now embraced co-creation as part of my working process regardless of different stages of design.
